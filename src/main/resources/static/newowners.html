Supplies

As with all pets, a list of supplies is needed. But what to buy? For a simple, quick to glance at list, you'll need food, bowls, a bed, toys, a collar, and a litterbox.

FOOD/BOWLS

All pets need to eat, and all pets need to eat well. Each cats dietary needs vary by cat. Some cats have allergies, some need more than others, and some can just be picky. A good tip to help them feel some sense of home is to try to feed them familiar foods at first. If the shelter/home you got them from gives the brand of food they feed their cats, consider using that for awhile and slowly introducing newer and potentially better foods. As long as you give them a diet that is fitting for their weight, whether it be dry, wet, or a mix of both, they should be fine.

As for bowls, make sure to not place their food and water bowl too close, make sure its wide enough so their whiskers don't touch the sides, and make sure to keep both their water and food FAR from the litterbox. Some cats prefer water fountains over water bowls, too!

Overall, the best thing you can do for your cats diet is to get to know your cat over time and their preferences.

BEDDING

Cats love comfy spots! They lounge a lot, so make sure their bed is very cozy for them! The only things to keep in mind is making sure its cozy and making sure its big enough.

Another structure they enjoy is cat trees. Felines love being up high, so getting them a safe, cozy cat tree will benefit them a lot. If you buy one you'll probably find them napping in it too!

TOYS

Toys are necessary for a cat! They help lower stress for them, gives them something to do, and lets you have an outlet to bond with them! It's a way to provide physical and mental enrichment for them! A good variety of toys is best. Get things such as toys you can play with together, like chasing/pouncing toys, as well as some individual play like catnip toys or puzzle ones.

Another type of toy that is ABSOLUTELY NECESSARY is some sort of scratching post! Cats LOVE to scratch, and giving them an outlet will save your furniture!

COLLAR

A collar is insanely important to have on your cat in case they get out. Make sure to get your pet a collar, preferably a bright or reflective one that makes them visible even in the dark, and a tag with your home address on it, contact info, and your cats name.

LITTERBOX

Cats need a litter box, or else they'll just go where they please. Buy a litter box bigger than your cat, and make sure to keep it far from where they eat. An important fact to keep in mind is that they will not use the litter box if it's too full, so empty it once or twice a day, and fully replace the sand once a week. The litter box should ideally be placed in a quiet, private area. It's important to be very mindful of how to handle a litter box, because if it's too full or in an area they won't want to use it, your cat will just go where they please on the floor.


Introducing Your Pet to Your Home


FIRST NIGHT

Although we know we'll be full of nothing but love to our new cat, the cat doesn't know this! Moving into a very unfamiliar home with some strange giant they've never met is terrifying! This is why the first night with them is important. The very first thing new owners should do when taking their new pet home is to give them space. Don't force them out of their carrier, just leave it open and let your new cat friend leave it and explore at their own pace!

Your new pet may be scared of you and run and hide, this is completely normal. It's important to not fight them on this. Trying to win their affection day one will only lengthen the process of getting them to trust you. For the first night/couple of nights, set up an area for them to sleep in. Enclose them in one room (the whole house can be overwhelming all at once!), preferably one you don't really frequent, and make sure to put their litterbox, toys, and bed in a nice, small space. Also make sure there are safe areas for them to hide in. All of this will allow them to feel comfortable and, over time, seeing you occasionally check in on the room peacefully will naturally build trust with them. Checking in occasionally over time will get the cat used to seeing you. You don't want to rush things!

NOTE: Some cats get comfortable and settled in minutes, and some take days. Usually kittens take a shorter time to adjust, but it really just depends on the individual cat.

    Things to Remember!
  • If you own plants, check if they're toxic to cats!!
  • Make sure doors/windows lock behind you. You don't want your new cat getting out, ESPECIALLY when its getting used to things.
  • Make sure not to leave food out!
  • When using things like the washer/dryer, check, check, and check again to make sure no cats are hiding inside before use. Some cats LOVE hiding in the washer/dryer.
  • DO NOT HANDLE THEM WITH YOUR HANDS TILL THEY'RE READY!
  • Make sure to close access to any places where your cat could squeeze in and get stuck.
  • Make sure to not keep out cords and strings that don't have to be out and to make sure to monitor cords that have to stay out (and very preferably, make them unaccessible). Some cats really like chewing on these.

PLACEMENT

When setting up your new cats room, make sure to place their bed, bowls, maybe a toy or two they can play with on their own, and their litterbox in the room. Make sure the litterbox is not too close to their bowls, as cats do not like their food being so close to where they use the bathroom. Also make sure to make some safe hiding spots for the cats to scurry into, and to block off any dangerous areas/breakables the cat could hurt themselves on. Make sure theres no areas the cat could get out of the room with and make sure to keep them far enough away from the door so they can't break out when you check in. As long as the cats eating and drinking, they'll adjust with time.


The Vet

The dreaded vet. No one likes going to the doctor, and no owner enjoys going to the vet, however, understanding when to go to the vet is a very important thing to know as a pet owner.

VACCINES

It's recommended to update your cats shots every three years!!!! Also look into what type of shot you give your cat, as some need boosters every year instead!

One of the very first things you should do when looking at adopting a cat is checking if its up to date on its vaccines. Only one vaccine is required by law, that being the Rabies shot, but to protect your pet to the fullest you should get all of them.

Some vaccines you should absolutely get your pet are the ones for Panleukopenia (or feline distemper) (FPV), feline calicivirus (FCV), Feline herpesvirus (FHV-1), and Feline viral rhinotracheitis (FVR). These are known as the "core" vaccines, as they are the most important to get to ensure your cat has a long, healthy life. You can knock three of them out with the FVRCP shot, which covers FVR, FCV, and FPV. This is also known as a "distemper" shot.

If your cat is a outdoor cat (although we do not recommend this), they should get more than this list. Shots they should get are the FELV shot (or the one for feline leukemia), Bordetella, and Chlamydia (though this one isn't recommended unless they've come into contact, as it can cause bad reactions). Even if your cat doesn't go outside, there's still a chance they get out, so we still highly recommend getting all your shots for your cat.

NOTE: a 4-in-1 shot also exists, which covers everything in FVRCP PLUS Chlamydia.

Your cat is considered a kitten until they're 1 year old, and should have vet visits more often during the first 16-20 weeks of their life. If you have a kitten, They should get their first round of vaccinations no earlier than 6 weeks, then should get shots every 3-4 weeks till they're around 16 weeks. The rabies shot is usually the last of these shots. Your kitten, during these visits, will also be dewormed and started on parasite preventives. After this, you'll start to develop more of an annual checkup schedule.

CHECKUPS

Having routine checkups for your cat is key to them living a long, healthy life! from ages 1-6 cats are considered adults, and 7-10 mature adults. ages 10 and up is considered senior.

For adult and mature adult cats, it's important to see the vet at least once a year. These annual vet visits can help your vet find possibly deadly conditions and stop them early. These checks ensure that your cat is up to date on their preventive care, and ensures that no unexpected medical conditions fly under the radar till it's too late. Cats like to hide their illnesses, so sometimes bad medical problems can go unseen till it becomes too much for the cat to hide.

When your cat becomes a senior, these annual checks should become once every 6 months. Being older, chronic health conditions are more likely to develop. Senior cat checkups will check for things like chronic kidney disease, cancer, diabetes, and hyperthyroidism, among other things.

EMERGENCIES

No one wants them to happen, but emergency medical situations do happen. To ensure you'll know where to go, research emergency vets in your area. As for what warrants an emergency? Things like:

  • Collapse or Seizure
  • Uncontrollable diarrhea or vomiting (+ a loss of appetite)
  • Traumatic injuries (a bone break, hit by a car, a really bad cut)
  • Difficulty Breathing
  • yowling in the litter box or difficulty urinating

Other things that would warrant an emergency vet visit is if you know for a fact your cat got into something deadly for it and ate a lot of it, or if your cat gets into a fight with an unknown potentially disease carrying cat.


FAQ!!

Still have questions? Maybe we can help here!

Animals go by scents a lot in nature. One of the best ways to initially introduce a new cat is to let it stay in its sanctuary room and to let your other pets wander outside. Occasionally swap it's bedding out and let your existing pets get a good sniff on it to help familiarize the new cats scent. You can also feed your pets on opposite sides of the door, as they will smell eachother while eating and associate eachothers scent with something good; food. After a few days, you can let them see eachother (without interacting physically yet), and after a bit of that, you can start to let them have supervised interactions. In short, just go slow.

If they're eating, it's not a problem. Some cats can be very skittish. If they aren't eating, however, it may be time to contact your local vet or animal behavior specialist. A good way to help your cat build trust is to be slow, sit in it's "sanctuary" room with toys and treats and let the cat come up to you. One of the best ways to help build trust is to keep a constant routine. This lets the cat get used to when it will see you and it will know your intent whenever it does see you.

Kittens are a lot more effort than adult cats. Check the vet section for specific information on how to handle their shots, but they also may require training that an adult cat may already come knowing how to do. Kittens are also a lot higher energy and will get into things (like cords, plants, etc) even more than an adult, so it's very important to supervise a kitten at all times. Kittens will also need more frequent feedings with specific kitten food and are overall more prone to injury and illness.

Cats in theory can survive outdoors. Some people's pet cats are outdoor cats and are allowed to come and go as they please! Although we don't personally recommend this, as it's not only a danger to other small animals outside but it's a danger to your cat, if you are going to have an outdoor cat, make sure they're microchipped, have a proper collar (that will break if the cats in danger), and have their claws. Also make sure your cat, if its going to be an outdoor cat, has all its vaccines! If you're just curious if you can take your indoors cat outside, they make cat harnesses to walk your cat with, and a fenced in area outside with no exit points won't do the cat much harm.

Stay calm, but still act. Cats can't always find their way home, so it's important to help them. Place things like their bedding or litter box near an exit point or outside so they have a higher chance of tracking a familiar scent. It's also highly recommended to use your community to your advantage. Make missing posters, inform your neighbors, search nearby. When searching yourself, bring some treats and search during a more quiet period of the day. Also contact your nearby shelters, as there is a good chance your cat could have been brought into one.

Generally an adult cat will be easier to take care of than a kitten. Kittens require a lot more vet visits and care, but an adult cat will probably already have its vet stuff for the most part sorted out, as well as it will probably already be trained.

Vets usually start this process when the kitten is around 5-6 months old. You don't want to do it too early, and you don't want to do it too late. If you got your cat from a shelter, chances are it was already spayed/neutered.
